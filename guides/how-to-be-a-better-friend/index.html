How to be a better friend

Being a better friend is rarely about dramatic gestures. It is usually about ordinary reliability: paying attention, following through, reaching out, and making the relationship easier to inhabit.

Updated June 22, 2026

Start the free 7-day challenge See the coaching practice

"Being a better friend is a practice problem, not a personality problem. You do not need a different character. You need more visible, consistent action."

Carole Stromboni

Being a better friend does not require a personality change. It requires more consistent action: following through on plans, remembering what matters to people, and reaching out when you think of someone instead of waiting for the right moment.

What I know after years of thinking about friendship is this: at the end of the day, what stays are the people. The trip fades. The job changes. The things you accumulated get sold or given away. The people, the ones you stayed close to, the ones who knew you across versions of yourself, those are what last. Becoming a better friend is one of the most worthwhile things you can work on, quietly and at any age.

Pay attention to what matters to the other person

A better friend notices. They remember something small, ask about the thing that mattered last week, and show that the other person's inner life did not disappear the moment the conversation ended.

You do not need perfect memory. You need enough attention to make the relationship feel real and not generic.

Professor Harry Reis at the University of Rochester developed the concept of perceived partner responsiveness, the experience of feeling that someone truly understands, validates, and cares about you. His research found that responsiveness is one of the strongest predictors of friendship quality and closeness. A better friend is not primarily more entertaining or more available. They are more consistently paying attention.

Parks and Recreation · NBC

In Parks and Recreation, Leslie Knope remembers everything about Ann. Her goals. Her struggles. Her small victories. Leslie is not always available. But she is always paying attention. Ann knows that what she shares with Leslie will not be forgotten by next week. That is what responsiveness looks like in practice.

Make friendship easier through follow-through

A lot of friendship trust comes from simple reliability. If you say you will text, text. If you suggest a plan, help make it real. If someone shares something important, circle back.

This sounds basic because it is basic. But adult friendships often strengthen or weaken around these small acts of follow-through.

Notice who is doing the pushing

In any friendship, there is often a push/pull dynamic at work. Whoever is initiating, sending the message, making the plan, following up, is pushing energy into the connection. Whoever is holding back or waiting is pulling.

If you are always the one pushing, it may signal an imbalance worth naming. If you are always pulling, it is worth asking whether you are showing up as the kind of friend you would want to have. The best friendships usually have both people willing to push.

Insecure · HBO

In Insecure, Issa and Molly take turns being the one who pulls while the other pushes. At different points in the series, one of them barely shows up while the other carries the friendship. What eventually works is not patience or space. It is a direct conversation about the friendship itself. Dr. Miriam Kirmayer calls this meta-communication: naming the dynamic instead of letting it quietly erode the connection.

Ask for help, it deepens friendship both ways

One of the most underused ways to deepen a friendship is to ask for something: advice, company in a hard moment, a favor, practical help. Asking signals that the relationship is real enough for vulnerability.

People who care about you usually want to be given a chance to show up. Asking for help gives them that chance, and often shifts the energy in friendships that have become one-directional.

Grace and Frankie · Netflix

In Grace and Frankie, two women who never would have chosen each other end up becoming real friends. Their bond does not grow through warmth or shared values. It grows through necessity. They keep needing each other in specific ways. They have to ask for help directly. That mutual dependency is what makes the relationship real. Admitting that you need someone is one of the fastest ways to deepen a friendship.

Do not wait for the other person to do all the maintenance

Many people secretly want mutual friendship while acting like initiators should always be the other person. A better friend helps carry the social weight of the relationship.

That can mean reaching out first, making a plan, checking in after a hard week, or being the one who keeps momentum alive.

Aim for friendships that lift both of you

Aristotle identified three kinds of friendship: friendships of pleasure, of utility, and of virtue, where both people share values and push each other toward their better selves. He considered the last kind the deepest.

A better friend is not just someone pleasant to be around. They are someone who reminds you, by example and honest presence, of who you are trying to become. Those friendships are rarer, but worth deliberately cultivating.

Ted Lasso · Apple TV+

In Ted Lasso, Rebecca and Keeley have almost nothing in common on the surface. What sustains their friendship is specific: each one invests in the other's growth. Rebecca encourages Keeley's ambition without competing with it. Keeley brings honesty into a space where Rebecca is guarded. They make each other's lives larger. That is what Aristotle called friendship of virtue.

Let care be practical, not only emotional

Being a good friend is not only about saying the right thing. It is also about showing up in usable ways: making time, remembering, asking, inviting, and staying present.

The Friendship Practice is built on that idea. Friendship improves when care becomes visible in real actions. If you want to go deeper on the maintenance side, read how to keep friends as an adult.

Research on what actually builds closeness in friendship points toward a specific kind of conversation that Professor Jeffrey Hall calls striving communication episodes: catching up on what has actually been happening, joking around to release tension, and engaging seriously when the moment calls for it. His data show that these forms of talk predict increased closeness over time, while an excess of small talk predicts the opposite. Being a better friend is partly a matter of resisting the social reflex to keep things polite and light, and choosing instead to be genuinely curious about what is real for the other person. (Journal of Social and Personal Relationships)

Shrinking · Apple TV+

In Shrinking, Paul's approach to Jimmy's grief is not gentle. He pushes back. He holds Jimmy accountable. He refuses to let Jimmy disappear into avoidance. But he makes it clear the investment is real. That combination of firmness and care is what Jeffrey Hall calls showing up in usable ways. It is not the most comfortable form of friendship. It is sometimes the most useful one.

Common questions

Quick answers

What makes someone a good friend? +

Usually a combination of warmth, reliability, attention, and follow-through. Good friendship is often built through repeatable care, not just strong feelings.

Can I improve at friendship even if I feel awkward? +

Yes. Friendship gets easier when you practice specific actions like reaching out, remembering, inviting, and following through. You do not need to become socially perfect to become a better friend.

How do I show care without it feeling forced? +

Start small and specific. Remember something the person mentioned last time. Ask about it. Send a message when you think of them. Care feels natural when it is specific, not when it is grand. Small acts of attention are more convincing than large gestures.

What should I do if I have been a bad friend recently? +

Acknowledge it simply and move forward. You do not need a long explanation. A short, honest message and a genuine next step are usually enough. People respond better to warmth and action than to elaborate apologies.

How do I support a friend going through something hard without saying the wrong thing? +

Ask before advising. Most people in difficulty want to feel heard, not fixed. Saying I am sorry, that sounds really hard, followed by do you want to talk about it or would you rather be distracted? is almost always the right starting point.

How do I stop being flaky and actually follow through on plans? +

Make fewer plans, not more. Overcommitting is the main driver of flakiness. When you say yes to something, put it in your calendar immediately with a reminder the day before. Intention without structure rarely survives a busy week.

What if I am a good friend to everyone but feel like no one really shows up for me? +

That is worth looking at carefully. Either the people around you are not capable of reciprocating, in which case the question is about who you are choosing to invest in. Or you are not making your needs visible, in which case the work is about asking for things explicitly, not just hoping they will be noticed.
