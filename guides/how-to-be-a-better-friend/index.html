How to be a better friend

Being a better friend is rarely about dramatic gestures. It is usually about ordinary reliability: paying attention, following through, reaching out, and making the relationship easier to inhabit.

Updated June 22, 2026

Many people want better friendships, but one of the clearest ways to get there is to become easier to be close to yourself. Friendship often grows through repeated signals of care, not through one perfect emotional moment.

What I know after years of thinking about friendship is this: at the end of the day, what stays are the people. The trip fades. The job changes. The things you accumulated get sold or given away. The people — the ones you stayed close to, the ones who knew you across versions of yourself — those are what last. Becoming a better friend is one of the most worthwhile things you can work on, quietly and at any age.

Pay attention to what matters to the other person

A better friend notices. They remember something small, ask about the thing that mattered last week, and show that the other person's inner life did not disappear the moment the conversation ended.

You do not need perfect memory. You need enough attention to make the relationship feel real and not generic.

Make friendship easier through follow-through

A lot of friendship trust comes from simple reliability. If you say you will text, text. If you suggest a plan, help make it real. If someone shares something important, circle back.

This sounds basic because it is basic. But adult friendships often strengthen or weaken around these small acts of follow-through.

Notice who is doing the pushing

In any friendship, there is often a push/pull dynamic at work. Whoever is initiating — sending the message, making the plan, following up — is pushing energy into the connection. Whoever is holding back or waiting is pulling.

If you are always the one pushing, it may signal an imbalance worth naming. If you are always pulling, it is worth asking whether you are showing up as the kind of friend you would want to have. The best friendships usually have both people willing to push.

Ask for help — it deepens friendship both ways

One of the most underused ways to deepen a friendship is to ask for something: advice, company in a hard moment, a favor, practical help. Asking signals that the relationship is real enough for vulnerability.

People who care about you usually want to be given a chance to show up. Asking for help gives them that chance, and often shifts the energy in friendships that have become one-directional.

Do not wait for the other person to do all the maintenance

Many people secretly want mutual friendship while acting like initiators should always be the other person. A better friend helps carry the social weight of the relationship.

That can mean reaching out first, making a plan, checking in after a hard week, or being the one who keeps momentum alive.

Aim for friendships that lift both of you

Aristotle identified three kinds of friendship: friendships of pleasure, of utility, and of virtue — where both people share values and push each other toward their better selves. He considered the last kind the deepest.

A better friend is not just someone pleasant to be around. They are someone who reminds you, by example and honest presence, of who you are trying to become. Those friendships are rarer, but worth deliberately cultivating.

Let care be practical, not only emotional

Being a good friend is not only about saying the right thing. It is also about showing up in usable ways: making time, remembering, asking, inviting, and staying present.

The Friendship Practice is built on that idea. Friendship improves when care becomes visible in real actions.

Common questions

Quick answers

What makes someone a good friend? +

Usually a combination of warmth, reliability, attention, and follow-through. Good friendship is often built through repeatable care, not just strong feelings.

Can I improve at friendship even if I feel awkward? +

Yes. Friendship gets easier when you practice specific actions like reaching out, remembering, inviting, and following through. You do not need to become socially perfect to become a better friend.

How do I show care without it feeling forced? +

Start small and specific. Remember something the person mentioned last time. Ask about it. Send a message when you think of them. Care feels natural when it is specific, not when it is grand. Small acts of attention are more convincing than large gestures.

What should I do if I have been a bad friend recently? +

Acknowledge it simply and move forward. You do not need a long explanation. A short, honest message and a genuine next step are usually enough. People respond better to warmth and action than to elaborate apologies.
