How to keep work friendships after going remote

Most work friendships are built on proximity and shared context. When you go remote, both disappear. The friendship does not have to go with them, but without deliberate effort, it usually does.

Updated July 1, 2026

"Most work friendships do not end when you go remote. They just stop having anywhere to happen. That is a solvable problem."

Carole Stromboni

Keeping work friendships after going remote is a specific challenge. It is different from maintaining a friendship that was always independent of work. The relationship formed in a context that no longer exists, and both people have to decide — usually without saying it out loud — whether to move it somewhere else. Most people never make that decision. The friendship fades quietly, and nobody is quite sure who let it go.

I have spoken to a lot of people who went remote and lost friendships they genuinely valued — not because the other person stopped liking them, but because the office had been doing most of the maintenance work all along. The ones who kept those friendships did one thing differently: they created a new structure instead of hoping the old one would survive.

The office was doing the work. Now you have to.

Work friendships run on repeated, unplanned contact: the coffee run, the walk to a meeting, the five minutes at the start of a call when the agenda has not started yet. None of it felt like effort because none of it was. It just happened.

Remote work removes all of that. What remains is a relationship that was sustained by a structure that no longer exists. Both people are busy. Both people have adjusted to working without the incidental contact. And so the friendship quietly stops.

Understanding this is the first useful step. You are not bad at friendship. The infrastructure that was doing the work for you has been removed. The question is whether you want to rebuild it.

Decide which friendships you actually want to keep

Not every work friendship needs to survive the transition. Some were sustained entirely by proximity and had no real substance beyond that. That is fine. Trying to maintain every office relationship will exhaust you and dilute the effort you put into the ones that matter.

Think about which people you genuinely liked, not just which ones you saw most. Who are the people you thought about when something interesting happened? Who did you miss in the first weeks of working from home? Those are the friendships worth investing in.

A short list of two or three people you actually want to maintain a real connection with is more useful than a long list of polite check-ins with everyone.

Create a structure to replace the one that disappeared

The most reliable way to keep a work friendship alive after going remote is to create a recurring touchpoint: a monthly virtual coffee, a shared lunch on video once in a while, a standing message thread that is not about work. It does not have to be formal. It just has to exist and repeat.

One-off plans that rely on someone remembering to suggest them tend to drift. A recurring slot removes the friction of constantly re-initiating. If it feels like too much to suggest a recurring call, start with one specific plan and see if it leads naturally to another.

The goal is contact often enough that the friendship does not have to be rebuilt from scratch each time. Once a month is usually enough. Once every three months is the minimum before it starts to feel like starting over.

A friendship does not need to be intense to stay alive. It needs to be consistent.

The real question: work friend or actual friend?

Going remote is a useful test. It forces both people to decide whether the friendship exists on its own terms or only in the context of work.

Some work friendships, when moved outside the office, reveal that you have genuine things in common: curiosity, values, humor, life situations. Those friendships can become something more durable than what you had before.

Others reveal that the shared context was doing most of the work. That is not a failure. It is useful information. You can appreciate what the friendship was without putting energy into something that was always more circumstantial than chosen.

If they stayed in the office and you went remote

Asymmetric remote arrangements are particularly hard on work friendships. The person still in the office has incidental contact with other colleagues; you do not. The friendship can start to feel like you are the only one making effort, because structurally, you are.

Name it lightly if you need to. Something like 'I know I miss the spontaneous stuff more than you probably do' acknowledges the asymmetry without making it a complaint. And then make one concrete suggestion for how to stay connected in a way that works for both of you.

If the other person is also making some effort, even small effort, the friendship can survive and sometimes deepen. If the effort is entirely one-sided over time, that is information too.

Common questions

Quick answers

How do I stay in touch with coworkers after going remote? +

Create a recurring touchpoint — a monthly call, a standing virtual coffee, a message thread that is not about work tasks. The goal is contact that happens regularly without requiring constant re-initiation. One specific standing plan is more reliable than open invitations that depend on someone remembering to suggest them.

Is it weird to reach out to work friends outside of work after going remote? +

Not at all. If you were genuinely close, a message that is clearly personal rather than work-related is almost always welcomed. Keep the first reach-out simple: mention you miss the incidental contact and suggest one specific thing, like a call or a catch-up.

How do I keep friendships with people still in the office when I work remotely? +

Acknowledge the asymmetry and work around it. They have built-in contact with other people; you do not. That means you will probably need to initiate more than they do, at least at first. Suggest a recurring touchpoint that fits both schedules and is explicitly social rather than work-related.

Why do work friendships fade when you go remote? +

Because most work friendships run on proximity and incidental contact — the coffee run, the walk between meetings, the informal conversations that happen without planning. Remote work removes all of that infrastructure. Without it, contact stops unless someone deliberately creates new structure to replace it.

How often should I check in with work friends when I am remote? +

Once a month is usually enough to keep a friendship alive without it feeling like maintenance. Once every three months is the minimum before each contact starts to feel like restarting from scratch. More than twice a month is only necessary if the friendship has genuine depth and both people are investing in it.
