How to meet people in a new city

Meeting people in a new city is rarely about one perfect event. It is usually about building repeated contact in places where you can become familiar, then taking the small social risks that turn familiarity into connection.

Updated June 22, 2026

Start the free 7-day challenge See the coaching practice

"You do not find friends in a new city. You become a regular somewhere, and then you find them."

Carole Stromboni

The fastest way to meet people in a new city is to become a regular somewhere. One activity, one place, shown up to consistently over several weeks. That repetition is what turns strangers into familiar faces, and familiar faces into friends.

I've moved between cultures and cities enough times to know that first feeling in a new place, surrounded by people, but with nobody to text. It's a specific kind of lonely, and it's very common. What helped me most wasn't finding the perfect event or the right group. It was just becoming a regular somewhere, showing up until my face was familiar, until 'oh, you're the one who...' started to happen. That's when things opened up.

Choose places where you can return

The fastest way to feel less alone in a new city is to stop chasing one-off encounters and start choosing repeatable environments. Classes, sports, volunteering, coworking spaces, language exchanges, recurring neighborhood events, and small communities work better than random big nights out.

Repetition matters because it lowers pressure. You do not need instant chemistry with anyone. You need enough repeated contact that a conversation can continue the next time.

Become a regular somewhere

A city becomes less anonymous when your face is recognized. That can happen at a cafe, a class, a market, a climbing gym, a studio, a bookstore event, or a walking group. Regularity creates familiarity, and familiarity makes social interaction easier.

This is often more effective than constantly searching for entirely new places. A repeated environment gives you more chances with the same people instead of always starting from zero.

Sociologist Ray Oldenburg described these repeatable spaces — the local café, the gym, the community center, the bookstore — as "third places": neutral ground outside of home and work where informal social connection has historically formed most naturally. His research argues that the erosion of third places in modern urban life is one of the primary structural causes of adult loneliness. (Third Places)

Turn warm moments into actual plans

Many people manage to meet others in a new city, but never turn those encounters into friendship because they wait too long after the good interaction. If something felt easy, follow up while it is still warm.

Keep it small and specific: another class next week, coffee after the event, a walk in the neighborhood, checking out the same market, or meeting before the next club session.

Once you have a few contacts, connect them to each other

One of the fastest ways to build a social life in a new city is to introduce the people you have already met to each other. If you know two people who would likely get along, connecting them creates something larger than two separate acquaintances.

Before making an introduction, check with each person separately, mention why you think they would connect and see if they are interested. If both are, make the introduction. This small act can seed a network that eventually sustains itself. For the full approach to making introductions well, see how to introduce friends to each other.

Accept that building a social life takes repetition

A new city can make you feel behind, but friendship usually grows slowly. The first phase is often logistical: showing up, recognizing faces, having short conversations, and becoming easier to place in other people's minds.

That is why practical friendship support matters. The shift is not from lonely to socially finished overnight. The shift is from waiting passively to creating conditions where connection can keep happening. For a broader look at how to make friends in any context, see how to make friends as an adult. If you have moved internationally, the added layer of cultural codes and language makes this harder — see how to make friends as an expat.

Common questions

Quick answers

How do I meet people if I just moved to a new city? +

Focus on repeatable places rather than one-off events. Choose environments you can return to, become a regular, and follow up quickly when an interaction feels warm.

Why is it so hard to make friends after moving? +

It is often hard because you have lost the routines and repeated contact that supported friendship before. In a new city, you usually have to rebuild those conditions on purpose.

How long does it take to feel settled socially in a new city? +

It usually takes longer than people expect, often six months to a year before a social life starts to feel genuinely comfortable. That is normal. The first phase is mostly logistical: showing up, recognizing faces, and becoming easier to place.

What if I work remotely and do not naturally meet many people? +

Remote work removes one of the most reliable sources of repeated adult contact. Replacing it requires choosing a recurring community outside of work: a class, a sport, a volunteer group, a coworking space, or any place you can return to consistently.

I go to events but never make real connections. What am I doing wrong? +

One-off events rarely produce friendships. The issue is not the event but the repetition. Go to the same event twice. Become a recognizable face. Friendships form when people see you again and remember you from before.

What is the fastest way to build a social life from scratch in a new place? +

Pick one recurring activity you genuinely enjoy and commit to it for at least two months. The goal is not to meet as many people as possible. It is to become a regular in one place until the faces start to feel familiar.

How do I meet people in a new city if I am introverted or find social events draining? +

Choose activities with a built-in task or focus, so the socializing happens around something. Classes, sports, volunteer work, book clubs. Interaction with a purpose is much easier to sustain than open-ended socializing.
