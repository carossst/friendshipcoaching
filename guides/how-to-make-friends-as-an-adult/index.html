How to make friends as an adult

Making friends as an adult is rarely about being more charismatic. It is usually about being more deliberate: putting yourself near compatible people, following up when something feels warm, and repeating contact before the connection goes cold.

Research by Professor Jeffrey Hall at the University of Kansas found it takes roughly 50 hours of shared time to move from acquaintance to casual friend, and around 200 hours for a close friendship. That number explains more than most friendship advice does: adult friendship is less about chemistry and more about repetition.

Making friends as an adult is possible, the method is simpler than most people expect: find a place where you can see the same person more than once, follow up after a warm exchange, and let the hours accumulate naturally.

I split my time between Hawaii and Paris, which means I've had to make new friends in new places more times than most people. And here's what I've learned: it's almost never about personality. It's about showing up to the same place twice and sending the message after a good conversation. You're not behind. You just haven't had a structure yet.

Start with places where repetition is normal

The easiest adult friendships usually begin where you see the same people more than once: classes, clubs, sports, volunteering, coworking spaces, recurring neighborhood events, or professional communities with a social edge.

One good conversation matters less than repeated light contact. Familiarity lowers awkwardness. It gives both people time to notice each other, recognize interest, and build trust without forcing intimacy too early. If you have recently moved and need to build those contexts from scratch, see how to meet people in a new city.

Research by Professor Jeffrey Hall at the University of Kansas found that it takes roughly 50 hours of shared time to move from acquaintance to casual friend, and around 200 hours to develop a close friendship. Those hours do not come from intensity, they accumulate through the ordinary repetition of showing up to the same place, again. (Journal of Social and Personal Relationships)

Most adult loneliness is not about missing people. It is about missing the repetition that turns people into friends.

Treat the follow-up as part of the friendship, not as a test

Many adults assume that if a connection were real, it would continue naturally. In practice, many good beginnings die because no one follows up. A message after meeting is not neediness. It is how adult friendship keeps moving.

Keep the follow-up concrete. Mention something specific you enjoyed, then suggest a small next step: coffee, a walk, another class, a game, a market, a lunch, a check-in next week.

Following up after a good conversation is not neediness. It is the single most underused friendship skill adults have.

Build from mutual interest, not from perfection

A promising friendship does not need instant closeness. It needs mutual goodwill, enough ease to try again, and enough structure to see each other another time.

If you wait for total certainty, you often miss the moment where friendship could have grown. Adult friendship usually starts as a series of modest invitations, not as a dramatic feeling.

Hacks · HBO Max

In Hacks, Deborah and Ava have nothing in common at the start. Deborah is a Las Vegas comedy legend in her sixties. Ava is a broke writer in her twenties. What creates the friendship is not choice or warmth. It is necessity and repeated time working together. They argue, collaborate, and slowly discover mutual respect. That is the model for adult friendship: not a perfect match from the start, but enough shared time to let the fit reveal itself.

The early phase is supposed to feel like trial and error

When you first start spending time with someone new, the connection often feels uneven. You are both figuring out what you like to talk about, what rhythm of contact works, what kind of friendship this might become. That calibration period is normal, not a warning sign.

Many adults read early awkwardness as proof that a connection will not work. Usually it just means the friendship is still new. Allowing some trial and error early on, rather than judging the connection too quickly, gives something real a chance to form.

Let friendship grow through practice

Friendship deepens through repeated acts: inviting, checking in, remembering something small, following through, showing up, and staying in contact after a good interaction.

That is the central idea behind The Friendship Practice: friendship is not just something you understand. It is something you do on purpose. If you want to understand why it feels so hard first, read why making friends as an adult is hard. Friendship is not something that falls into your lap. It's something you practice.

Common questions

Quick answers

Why is making friends as an adult so hard? +

It is often harder because adulthood offers less built-in time, less repetition, and more competing responsibilities. The solution is usually not to wait for friendship to happen, but to create more intentional contact and follow-through.

How do I turn a good conversation into a friendship? +

Follow up while the interaction is still warm. Mention something specific you liked, then suggest one small next step. Adult friendship usually grows through repetition, not through one strong first impression.

What do I do if I am shy or find it hard to initiate? +

Start with environments where repeated contact happens naturally, so you do not have to initiate from zero. A recurring class or group gives you a reason to be there again without a formal invitation. Small follow-ups are easier than big ones.

How long does it take to make a real friend as an adult? +

Research suggests it can take 50 hours or more of contact to move from acquaintance to casual friend, and more to feel close. That number sounds large, but it accumulates through repeated light contact over months, not through long intense sessions.

I had a great first conversation with someone but do not know how to follow up. What should I do? +

Send a short message within a day or two. Reference something specific from your conversation and suggest a next step: a coffee, a walk, whatever fits. The message does not need to be clever. It needs to exist.

How do I make friends when I work from home and rarely meet people in person? +

Remote work removes the easiest structure for repeated contact. You have to create it deliberately: a regular class, a club, a coworking space, an online community you show up in consistently. The goal is repetition, not one-off events.

Is it weird to ask someone to hang out when we have only just met? +

No. It is actually the move most people want someone to make but nobody does. A simple invite after a warm exchange is not forward. It is what turns a good encounter into something real.

What if I feel like I am just bad at making friends? +

Most people who describe themselves as bad at making friends have never been given a method, only the conditions that made friendship automatic when they were younger. What feels like a personality deficit is almost always a structural gap. The approach in this guide is learnable, not innate. You are not behind. You just have not had a structure yet.
