How to make friends as an expat

Making friends as an expat is harder than making friends in a new city at home. You are navigating language, cultural codes, an absence of shared references, and the particular loneliness of starting over in a place where everyone already has their people.

"Expat loneliness is not about being in the wrong place. It is about being in the right place without enough repetition yet."

Carole Stromboni

Making friends as an expat is possible, and the method is more deliberate than most people expect. You cannot rely on organic connection. You have to create the conditions for it: find recurring contexts, invest in them consistently, follow up quickly, and give things more time than you would at home.

Splitting my time between Hawaii and Paris means I have done this many times, in both directions. What I have learned: expat friendship does not come from one great event or one expat meetup. It comes from showing up to the same place for the third, fourth, fifth time. The connection starts forming before you realize it.

Why making friends as an expat is its own challenge

At home, you build social connection on a foundation that already exists: shared language, shared cultural references, familiar codes for friendliness and distance, an existing network that creates introductions. None of that is automatic when you arrive somewhere new.

Locals often have full social lives and less urgency to add new people. Other expats may be in survival mode or planning to leave. The usual social shortcuts do not work the same way.

This is not a reason to give up. It is a reason to be more patient and more deliberate than you would need to be at home.

Other expats are an easier starting point

Other expats share your situation: they are also building a social life from scratch, they are often more open to new connection, and they understand the particular mix of excitement and loneliness that comes with living abroad.

Expat communities, international clubs, language exchanges, and online groups for people in the same city are all reasonable starting points. The goal is not to build a friendship group made entirely of expats, but to have a social base while you put down deeper roots.

Use the expat community as a launching pad, not a permanent destination.

Find recurring contexts in local life

The friendships that last longest for expats are often with locals, because locals tend to stay. Building those friendships takes more time and more deliberate effort, but it is worth prioritizing alongside connections with other expats.

The most reliable way to meet locals is through repeated shared activity: a sports team, a class, a club, a volunteer project, a professional community with a social dimension. Something where you see the same faces week after week.

Language is a real barrier in some countries and worth addressing directly. Even basic effort in the local language is usually met with warmth and signals genuine respect for where you are.

Most adult loneliness is not about missing people. It is about missing the repetition that turns people into friends.

Lower your expectations for the timeline

Expat friendship often feels slower than friendship at home. People are more guarded, the social codes are different, and building trust across cultural difference takes time.

What feels like failure at three months is often just the normal arc of expat connection. The friendships that form after six months to a year are usually more solid than the ones that feel instant but don't survive your first difficult period abroad.

Give the process more time than feels comfortable. Most expats who later say they built a real social life abroad also say it took longer than they expected.

Do not wait to be invited

One of the most common expat mistakes is waiting to be included. The expectation that locals or other expats will notice you are new and fold you in rarely holds.

You usually have to be the one who reaches out, suggests the coffee, follows up after a good conversation, and keeps creating the next moment. This feels like a lot of effort at first. After a few months, it starts to feel like your social life.

Common questions

Quick answers

How do I make friends as an expat when I do not speak the local language well? +

Start with English-speaking expat communities while you build language skills. Even small efforts in the local language signal respect and open doors. Many locals will appreciate the attempt even if conversations happen in English.

How long does it take to make real friends as an expat? +

Typically longer than at home. Expect 6 to 12 months before friendships feel genuinely solid. The early months usually involve a lot of surface-level connection that gradually deepens through repeated contact. This is normal, not a sign that it is not working.

Should I focus on expats or locals when trying to make friends abroad? +

Both, ideally. Other expats are easier to connect with initially and understand your situation. Locals provide roots, deeper cultural insight, and tend to be more stable when expats come and go. Build both kinds of connection over time.

I have been abroad for a year and still feel lonely. What am I doing wrong? +

Probably nothing wrong, but possibly not enough repetition. Ask yourself: am I showing up to the same places consistently? Am I following up after good conversations? Am I suggesting specific things rather than waiting to be included? Loneliness this far in is usually a sign that the connection exists but the maintenance is missing.

How do I maintain friendships back home while building new ones abroad? +

Use the same principles: short, regular contact beats infrequent long catch-ups. A quick message, a shared link, a brief voice note. Your home friendships will not maintain themselves, but they also do not require a lot if you keep showing up in small ways.
