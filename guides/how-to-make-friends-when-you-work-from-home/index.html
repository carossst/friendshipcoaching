How to Make Friends When You Work From Home

Making friends when you work from home is harder because remote work eliminates the most reliable source of repeated contact adults have: the workplace. The solution is not to replicate the office. It is to build new structures on purpose.

Updated June 29, 2026

"Remote work is not a social life problem. It is a structure problem. The office was doing most of the social infrastructure work without you having to think about it. Now you have to build that infrastructure yourself."

Before remote work, the office did a lot of the friendship infrastructure work without anyone having to think about it. You saw the same people every day, conversations happened naturally, and some of them became real. When that structure disappears, the social life built on it disappears with it.

I have spent most of my adult working life without a traditional office, splitting time between Hawaii and Paris and working independently. What I know from that: the social life does not build itself. You have to choose where to show up, and then actually show up.

The real problem with remote work and friendship

The office worked for social connection not because coworkers are particularly well-matched friends, but because it created repeated contact automatically. You saw the same people daily, had small conversations, shared complaints and jokes, and some of those interactions accumulated into genuine closeness.

Remote work removes that repetition. And without repetition, acquaintances stay acquaintances. The warmth from a good video call does not compound the way physical presence does.

The fix is not to find a perfect community or meet the right people. It is to engineer the repetition that remote work removed.

A related finding from Professor Jeffrey Hall's research helps explain this more precisely. His data show that time spent in what he calls "closed systems" — environments like offices or classrooms where people are grouped by obligation rather than choice — is a significantly weaker predictor of friendship closeness than freely chosen time. Hours at the office were never converting reliably into deep friendship. What the office provided was a scaffold: repeated proximity that made freely chosen moments — a spontaneous lunch, a conversation after a meeting — more likely. Remote work removed the scaffold. The task for remote workers is not to replicate the office. It is to build the voluntary, recurring contact that the office was masking as inevitable. (Journal of Social and Personal Relationships)

Research on communication and workplace social dynamics consistently shows that what remote work primarily eliminates is not collaboration but unplanned, ambient contact — the spontaneous exchange in a hallway, the conversation before a meeting starts, the brief encounter at the coffee machine. These low-stakes, unscheduled interactions are what build social familiarity over time. Research published in the Harvard Business Review has found that remote workers lose a significant portion of these informal touchpoints, which are disproportionately responsible for building interpersonal trust. (Harvard Business Review)

Choose one recurring context and commit to it

The most effective move for remote workers is to pick one activity that happens on a regular schedule and show up consistently for at least two months. A weekly class, a sports league, a coworking space on the same days each week, a local club.

The goal is not to meet as many people as possible. It is to become a recognizable face in one place. Friendship starts when someone sees you again and remembers you from before. That cannot happen at one-off events.

Choose something you would enjoy even if you made no friends from it. That removes the pressure and makes the repetition sustainable.

The social life you want will not find you at home. It will find you at the same place, at the same time, for the third week in a row.

Use coworking spaces strategically

A coworking space gives you the basic infrastructure the office once provided: a shared space, familiar faces, and small daily interactions. The key is regularity. Going once will not do anything. Going every Tuesday and Thursday for a month starts to build something.

You do not need to be aggressively social. Saying hello, being available for small exchanges, and occasionally suggesting a coffee with someone you have chatted with a few times is enough. The space does most of the work if you show up consistently.

Even one or two days a week in a shared space can dramatically change how isolated remote work feels.

Online communities can be a starting point, not an endpoint

Online communities, whether Slack groups, Discord servers, or forums around a shared interest, can be a genuine source of connection for remote workers. The key is to eventually move conversations offline or at least to direct, personal contact.

The people worth knowing in those communities are usually the ones who are active, thoughtful, and interested in people beyond the topic. A direct message that references something they said and asks a genuine question is a reasonable way to start.

If you are in a city with other members of an online community you value, suggesting an occasional meetup or coworking session is a natural bridge between online and real-world connection.

Common questions

Quick answers

I work from home and feel isolated. Is this normal? +

Very normal and very common. Remote work removes the background social infrastructure that most people did not realize they were relying on. The isolation is not a personal failing. It is a structural problem that requires a structural solution.

How do I make friends as a remote worker without forcing it? +

Choose a recurring context you genuinely enjoy, show up consistently, and let conversations develop naturally over time. Do not go in trying to make friends. Go in to do the activity. The social part follows from repetition, not from effort in the moment.

Can I make real friends through online communities? +

Yes, especially when the community is built around a genuine shared interest and you invest in it consistently. The friendships that develop online and then move to direct, personal contact, whether video calls or in-person meetings, are often very real.

What if I am introverted and find social settings draining even when I know I need connection? +

Choose contexts with a built-in task or focus. Classes, sports, volunteer work, or a coworking space where you are also working are much easier for introverts than open social events. The interaction has a natural container and a natural end time, which makes it far more sustainable.

I had a good social life before I went remote. How do I rebuild it? +

The foundation is already there: you know you can connect with people. What changed is the structure that made it easy. Identify what that structure was, what you were doing regularly that put you around people, and build a new version of it deliberately.
